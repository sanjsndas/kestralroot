☁️ rooted in sky: our mission

Kestral Root Lab was founded in 1973 by botanist and colour theorist Dr. Helena Kestral. Her radical idea: that the colour blue could fundamentally alter plant resilience and human well‑being. What began as a small hillside trial with chicory and blue flax has grown into a 28‑acre research campus, recognised globally for breakthroughs in chroma‑cultivation, cloud‑capture hydrology, and mycelial network enhancement. We are a non‑profit institute dedicated to open‑source gardening knowledge and the preservation of blue‑hued heritage plants. Our mission is to deepen the connection between sky and soil, and to demonstrate that gardening with intention — especially with the colour blue — can restore ecosystems and calm minds.

🧪 2. the founder’s story: helena kestral (1935–2010)

Helena grew up in the foggy moors of Vermont, where she collected gentians and dreamed of the azure Mediterranean. After earning a PhD in plant physiology, she travelled to the Himalayas to study Meconopsis (blue poppy) in situ. She returned with seeds and a conviction: blue flowers possessed a unique energy. In 1973 she established the Root Lab on an abandoned dairy farm. With a single greenhouse and a team of two, she began cross‑breeding blue‑flowered perennials and documenting the effects of blue‑reflective mulches. By 1985 the lab had released 14 new cultivars, including Delphinium ‘Kestral Sky’ and Baptisia ‘True Blue’. Her 1998 book Cerulean Ground remains a cult classic. Today, her legacy continues through our fellowship program.

🌿 3. where we are: bluefield campus

Our headquarters sit at 1470 Skyline Drive, Bluefield, Vermont — a valley known for its clean air and high cloud cover. The campus includes six research greenhouses (three with blue‑tinted glazing), a temperature‑controlled seed bank (2,800 accessions), the “cumulus” outdoor trial gardens, and the Skye library, which holds 5,000 volumes on colour in nature. We also maintain a 12‑acre satellite station in the drier climate of eastern Oregon, focusing on blue‑hued succulents and drought‑tolerant salvias. Visitors are welcome year‑round; the cloud‑capture rain garden and meditation labyrinth (painted in seven shades of blue) are open daily until dusk. The lab’s iconic water tower, painted Kestral blue, can be seen from the interstate.

📊 4. research pillars & key findings

Our work is organised into four pillars: ① chroma‑cultivation – how blue light and blue pigments affect plant growth (we’ve published 80+ peer‑reviewed papers). ② mycelial networks – studying indigo‑staining fungi and their role in carbon transfer. ③ cloud‑capture water systems – biomimicry of stratus clouds for irrigation. ④ human‑plant interaction – colour psychology in garden design. Among the major findings: blue light at 470nm increases stomatal conductance by 18% in lettuce; Lactarius indigo mycelium enhances phosphorus uptake in oaks; and gardens with 30% blue flowers reduce visitor heart rate by an average of 5bpm. We also developed the “Kestral Blue Index” (KBI) to quantify flower blueness, used by breeders worldwide.

🧑‍🔬 5. meet the team (sky‑blue minds)

Today’s team consists of 23 scientists, gardeners, and educators led by director Dr. Yuki Harada (plant physiology) and head curator Eliza Thorne (taxonomy). Dr. Harada’s team recently mapped the genome of Meconopsis ‘Kestral Strain’, identifying the delphinidin pathway genes. Eliza manages the seed bank and leads the annual “blue expeditions” — collecting trips to the Balkans, Andes, and South Africa. Our extension educators, Maria and Carlos, run workshops for schools and community gardens. We also host 6–8 resident researchers each year through the Kestral Fellowship. The entire staff meets every Friday for a “cloud hour” — walking the gardens and sharing observations.

📚 6. seed bank & conservation

The Kestral Root Lab Seed Bank is a founding member of the Blue Crop Conservancy. We prioritise species with blue‑pigmented flowers, fruits, or foliage, especially those threatened in their native habitats. Our cold vaults (‑20°C) currently hold 2,847 accessions, including wild collected Gentiana from the Alps, Eryngium from Patagonia, and Lobelia tupa from Chile. We supply seeds to botanic gardens and research institutions at no cost. For home gardeners, we release an annual “Sky Selections” list — 50 varieties with detailed germination codes. In 2025 we will launch the “Adopt a Blue” program, allowing supporters to sponsor conservation of a particular species.

⏳ 7. timeline: five decades of sky

1973 – Helena Kestral incorporates the lab. 1981 – first cloud‑capture system built. 1988 – introduction of Baptisia ‘Blue Pearl’. 1993 – lab expands to 20 acres. 2000 – blue light/root hair discovery. 2005 – non‑profit status. 2010 – Helena passes away; board appoints first external director. 2015 – launch of “Kestral Blue Index”. 2020 – online seed bank database goes live. 2024 – groundbreaking for the new Sky Education Center. Each milestone represents a deepening of our connection to blue.

🪴 8. public programs & visiting

We offer guided tours every Saturday at 10am (cloud‑capture walk) and 1pm (blue border focus). The “sky lab” is open for self‑guided visits Tue–Sun, 9am–6pm (May–Oct). Admission is free, but donations support our seed bank. We also run a monthly webinar series: topics include “propagating blue poppies”, “edible blues”, and “garden colour theory”. On the third Thursday of each month, join our “blue hour” — an evening of music, iced tea with borage, and twilight garden strolls. School groups are always welcome; we provide activity sheets aligned with STEM standards. Private workshops (e.g. natural dyeing, seed saving) can be booked through our contact page.

📘 9. selected publications & open access

All research conducted at Kestral Root Lab is published under Creative Commons licenses. Key titles: “Reflected blue light increases secondary metabolites in basil” (2023), “Mycelial networks under azurite‑amended soils” (2024), “Cloud‑capture efficiency of different fabrics” (2022). Our library also hosts the “Blue Gardeners’ Almanac”, an annual compilation of observations from 200+ volunteer monitors across North America and Europe. Every paper and datasheet is downloadable from our website (kestralrootlab.org/open). We believe that knowledge should flow like water — freely.
